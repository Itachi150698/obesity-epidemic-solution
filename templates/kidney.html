Kidney Disease Prediction

Before we explore the features for predicting kidney disease, let's understand how these features contribute to assessing the risk of kidney-related issues. Each feature provides valuable insights that aid in evaluating the overall condition of the kidneys.

  • Age: The age of the individual. Ranges from 0 to 30 years (Low risk) and 31+ years (High risk).
  • Blood Pressure: The blood pressure of the individual. Ranges from 60-80 mmHg (Low risk) and 80+ mmHg (High risk).
  • Specific Gravity: The specific gravity of urine. Ranges from 1.000 to 1.010 (Low risk) and 1.010+ (High risk).
  • Albumin: The level of albumin in the urine. Low (0-1) and High (1+). Elevated values may indicate kidney issues.
  • Sugar: The presence of sugar in the urine. Low (0) and High (1+). Elevated levels may be associated with kidney dysfunction.
  • Blood Glucose Random: The random blood glucose level. Low (70-120 mg/dL) and High (120+ mg/dL). Elevated levels may indicate diabetes.
  • Blood Urea: The concentration of urea in the blood. Low (10-50 mg/dL) and High (50-100 mg/dL). Elevated levels may indicate potential kidney dysfunction.
  • Serum Creatinine: The level of creatinine in the blood. Low (0.1-5 mg/dL) and High (5-10 mg/dL). Higher values can suggest impaired kidney function.
  • Sodium: The sodium level in the blood. Low (120-135 mEq/L) and High (135+ mEq/L). Abnormal levels may impact kidney function.
  • Potassium: The potassium level in the blood. Low (3.5-5 mEq/L) and High (5+ mEq/L). Abnormal levels may affect kidney health.
  • Haemoglobin: The amount of hemoglobin in the blood. Low (1-5 g/dL) and High (15-20 g/dL). Abnormal levels may be associated with kidney issues.
  • Packed Cell Volume: The proportion of blood volume occupied by red blood cells. Low (24-36%) and High (36+ %). Abnormal levels may indicate kidney disorders.
  • White Blood Cell Count: The number of white blood cells per microliter of blood. Low (0-5000 cells/µL) and High (5000-10000 cells/µL). Deviations may indicate infection or inflammation in the kidneys.
  • Red Blood Cell Count: The number of red blood cells per microliter of blood. Low (2-6 million cells/µL) and High (6-10 million cells/µL). Abnormal levels may be linked to kidney disorders.
  • Hypertension: Indicates the presence (High risk) or absence (Low risk) of hypertension.
  • Diabetes Mellitus: Indicates the presence (High risk) or absence (Low risk) of diabetes mellitus.

Understanding the ranges and descriptions of these features is crucial for assessing the risk of kidney disease. Here's a general guideline for risk levels:

  • Low Risk: Values within the lower range of each feature.
  • High Risk: Values at the upper range, indicating a significant risk of kidney disease.
