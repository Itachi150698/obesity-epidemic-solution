Before we delve into diabetes features, let's understand how these features play a crucial role in predicting diabetes risk. Each feature provides valuable information that contributes to the overall assessment.

  • Pregnancies: The number of times a person has been pregnant. Ranges from 0 to 17.
  • Glucose: The blood glucose concentration. Ranges from 0 to 199. Higher values may indicate higher diabetes risk.
  • Blood Pressure: The diastolic blood pressure. Ranges from 0 to 122. Elevated values may suggest health concerns.
  • Skin Thickness: The thickness of the skinfold. Ranges from 0 to 99. Higher values may be associated with higher body fat.
  • Insulin: Insulin concentration. Ranges from 0 to 846. Abnormal values may indicate insulin resistance.
  • BMI (Body Mass Index): A measure of body fat based on height and weight. Ranges from 0 to 67.1. Higher values may indicate obesity.
  • Diabetes Pedigree Function: A function that represents diabetes prevalence in relatives. Ranges from 0 to 2.42. Higher values may indicate a genetic predisposition.
  • Age: The age of the person. Ranges from 21 to 81. Age can be a significant factor in diabetes risk assessment.

Understanding the ranges and descriptions of these features is crucial for assessing diabetes risk. Here's a general guideline for risk levels:

  • Low Risk: Values within the lower range of each feature.
  • High Risk: Values at the upper range, indicating a significant risk of diabetes. For specific feature ranges:
    • Pregnancies: 5-17
    • Glucose: 126-199
    • Blood Pressure: 80-122
    • Skin Thickness: 30-99
    • Insulin: 150-846
    • BMI (Body Mass Index): 25-67.1
    • Diabetes Pedigree Function: 0.8-2.42
    • Age: 40-81
