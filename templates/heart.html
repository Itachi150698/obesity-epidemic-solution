Before we delve into heart disease features, let's understand how these features play a crucial role in predicting heart disease risk. Each feature provides valuable information that contributes to the overall assessment.

  • Sex: Gender of the person (0 for Female, 1 for Male).
  • Chest Pain Type: Type of chest pain (1: Typical Angina, 2: Atypical Angina, 3: Non-Anginal Pain, 4: Asymptomatic).
  • Resting Blood Pressure (mmHg): The diastolic blood pressure.
  • Cholesterol (mg/dL): Cholesterol level in mg/dL.
  • Fasting Blood Sugar > 120 mg/dL: 1 if true, 0 if false.
  • Resting Electrocardiographic Results: 0 for Normal, 1 for ST-T Wave Abnormality, 2 for Probable or Definite Left Ventricular Hypertrophy.
  • Maximum Heart Rate Achieved: Maximum heart rate achieved during exercise.
  • Exercise Induced Angina: 1 if Yes, 0 if No.
  • ST Depression Induced by Exercise Relative to Rest: ST depression induced by exercise relative to rest.
  • Slope of the Peak Exercise ST Segment: 0 for Upsloping, 1 for Flat, 2 for Downsloping.
  • Number of Major Vessels Colored by Fluoroscopy: Number of major vessels colored by fluoroscopy (0-3).
  • Thalassemia: 1 for Normal, 2 for Fixed Defect, 3 for Reversible Defect.

Understanding the ranges and descriptions of these features is crucial for assessing heart disease risk. Here's a general guideline for risk levels:

  • Low Risk: Values within the lower range of each feature.
  • High Risk: Values at the upper range, indicating a significant risk of heart disease.
